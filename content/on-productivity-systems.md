Title: On Productivity Systems
Date: 2016-01-05
status: draft

People are naturally lazy. Sometimes this can be a good thing, like when it
leads to automation. But it can also lead to severely reduced productivity,
because procrastination is a constant danger. This is where systems come in.
Systems can act as a binary choice. When it comes to deciding what to do in a
particular moment, there are many different nuances between working and
procrastinating. When using a system, you either follow the system or you do
not, and not following the system is a conscious choice against being
productive.

Almost every programmer knows what it means to feel the flow. That weird feeling
you get at two in the morning while working on a side-project, when the the time
flies by and suddenly all important features are done. The reason why this
happens is because you already know what you want to do and how to do it.

Most established systems like GTD or Pomodoro use a designated planning phase to
divide tasks into manageable chunks. What this does is it tries to stimulate the
flow by forcing you to sit down and think about what and how you want to
accomplish, and then providing you with a simple checklist to work on
sequentially. TDD, which I have been writing about before several times,
integrates well with these more universal systems, as test cases often map one
to one to tasks.
